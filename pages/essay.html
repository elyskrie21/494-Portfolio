Ethics in Engineering: A Reflection on Purpose, Decision-Making, and Virtues

Ethics is fundamental in engineering, guiding professionals in their actions and decisions. A "Code of Ethics" serves as a moral compass, providing a set of principles and values that engineers should uphold in their practice. This essay will explore the purpose of having and following a Code of Ethics, delve into the personal decision-making process in ethical situations, discuss ethical issues addressed in a class, and reflect on the "Virtue of Ethics" concerning a case study.

A "Code of Ethics" in engineering serves multiple crucial purposes. Firstly, it establishes ethical standards, fostering trust and credibility among professionals and the public. It outlines acceptable behavior, ensuring that engineers maintain integrity, professionalism, and accountability. Secondly, it guides engineers when they encounter complex ethical dilemmas by providing a reference point for ethical decision-making. For instance, the IEEE Code of Ethics sets forth principles such as "to hold paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the public" and "to avoid real or perceived conflicts of interest." These principles help engineers navigate challenging situations with clarity.

My decision-making process involves several key factors when faced with an ethical situation. Firstly, I assess the situation and identify the ethical dilemma at hand. Next, I consider the potential consequences of various courses of action, both in terms of benefits and harm. Factors such as the impact on family members and friends, societal implications, and legal obligations are considered. Furthermore, I evaluate the principles I have been taught by my parents and other aspects of my life relevant to the situation, seeking guidance from established ethical norms. Additionally, I engage in ethical discussions with friends, family, and co-workers, seeking diverse perspectives to inform my decisions. Ultimately, my decision is based on a balance of these considerations, striving to uphold the highest ethical standards.

During our ethics in engineering class, we delved into various topics, including environmental responsibility, safety concerns, professional integrity, and conflicts of interest. We gained valuable insight into the ethical dilemmas engineers may encounter in their professional practice by examining real-world case studies.

The ethical considerations introduced in the class were both similar and different from my ethics. They were similar in that they emphasized the importance of safety, public welfare, and integrity, aligning with principles within an established code of ethics (While the class focused on the IEEE Code of Ethics, I used my “own” form of code of ethics). However, the class discussions often introduced nuances and complexities specific to engineering practice, challenging me to think critically about applying ethical principles. While my ethics provided a solid foundation, the class discussions enriched my understanding of the ethical dimensions unique to the engineering profession.

In our class, reaching a consensus on the "most ethical" decision could be challenging, as ethical dilemmas often involve multiple perspectives and trade-offs. However, the discussions were invaluable in highlighting the importance of ethical deliberation and the need to consider diverse viewpoints. While consensus was only sometimes achieved, the process of ethical discourse was informative and encouraged critical thinking.

In the context of the Grimshaw case, three virtues from the "Virtue of Ethics" stand out as particularly relevant:

  1. Integrity: Integrity is essential because it involves being truthful and transparent about product safety issues. In the Grimshaw case, Ford's lack of transparency regarding Pinto’s safety concerns was a significant ethical failing.
  2. Responsibility: Responsibility is closely tied to ensuring the safety of the products engineer’s design and manufacture. Engineers have a moral obligation to prioritize public safety over profit, which was a key issue in the Grimshaw case.
  3. Honesty: Honesty is vital in acknowledging and addressing safety issues honestly, both within the organization and with the public. Ford's failure to be honest about Pinto’s safety issues had serious ethical consequences.

In the "Virtue of Ethics," the other virtues are important in different situations, but they are especially relevant to the Grimshaw case because they relate directly to product safety and corporate responsibility. In addition to the ethical virtues mentioned in the "Virtue of Ethics," courage and fairness also apply to this case study. It required courage to stand up for what is just when facing a major corporation, and fairness was crucial in making decisions that didn't overly favor one party while neglecting the rights and interests of others.

Engineering professionals navigate a complex landscape of ethical dilemmas. A "Code of Ethics" provides guidance and principles, while personal decision-making processes involve assessing dilemmas, considering consequences, and seeking guidance from established norms. Ethical issues discussed in class enhance our understanding of professional ethics, even as they introduce unique perspectives and complexities. The "Virtue of Ethics" framework offers valuable insights into the virtues that engineers should embody, with integrity, responsibility, and honesty particularly relevant to our case study. Ethical reflection and discourse remain essential for engineers to uphold the highest ethical standards and make responsible decisions that prioritize the well-being of society and the profession.

General Education Reflection – Iowa State University

Throughout my time at Iowa State University, the general education courses I’ve taken have significantly shaped the way I think, communicate, and engage with problems—especially those I may encounter as an engineer. Classes like LIB160, ARCH321, ENGL150, and ENGL250 have broadened my perspective beyond technical knowledge, offering valuable tools for research, communication, and understanding the societal context in which engineering solutions exist.

LIB160 was my first step into learning how to access and evaluate credible information—a critical skill in both academic and professional settings. This course taught me how to efficiently use library databases, assess sources for reliability, and cite them appropriately. These research skills are fundamental to solving engineering problems, where backing up technical decisions with reliable data can be crucial. Whether I’m writing a report or validating a proposed design, the ability to locate and assess supporting information ensures my work is thorough and informed.

In ARCH321, the History of the American City, I explored how urban environments have developed and changed over time. This class gave me a better understanding of the social, environmental, and economic factors that influence city planning and infrastructure. By studying the evolution of American cities, I now have a greater appreciation for how engineering decisions—such as building design, transportation systems, and zoning—impact communities long-term. This historical insight is invaluable when thinking about sustainable and inclusive solutions to contemporary issues like climate change and urban sprawl.

ENGL150 helped me develop critical thinking and argumentative writing skills. Through essays, peer reviews, and class discussions, I learned how to construct well-reasoned arguments and communicate my ideas clearly. As an engineering student, this has translated directly into how I approach problems: identifying key issues, considering different perspectives, and presenting logical solutions. Strong communication is essential in engineering teams, and ENGL150 laid the groundwork for articulating technical and ethical concerns with clarity.

Building on that foundation, ENGL250 focused on technical communication—an area directly aligned with engineering. In this course, I learned how to write formal reports, create professional documents, and adapt language for various audiences. Whether it’s a design proposal or an instruction manual, technical communication is a daily part of engineering practice. ENGL250 taught me how to tailor my writing to both technical and non-technical readers, ensuring my ideas are understood by stakeholders at all levels.

Collectively, these general education courses have not only enriched my education but also equipped me to tackle complex engineering challenges. They’ve helped me think critically, communicate effectively, and consider the broader social and environmental context of the work I’ll do. For example, as climate change continues to affect communities globally, my ability to research solutions, understand historical trends, and communicate designs clearly will be essential in creating sustainable infrastructure.

In conclusion, general education at Iowa State has provided me with a strong, interdisciplinary foundation that complements my technical training. By integrating research skills, historical awareness, critical thinking, and communication, I am better prepared to face real-world engineering problems with a holistic approach.

A Reflective Journey: Navigating My Cumulative Experience at Iowa State

Computer engineering is a field that merges elements of electrical engineering and computer science to design and develop advanced computing technologies. While the technical aspects of hardware and software design stand at the core of this discipline, my journey at Iowa State University has shown me that a broader perspective is essential to becoming a well-rounded engineer. Courses in communication, the arts, and social sciences have deepened my understanding of engineering’s impact on society and prepared me for the multidisciplinary realities of the professional world.

Part I: Impact of General Education Courses on Professional Development

1. Beyond the Technical Aspects of Problem-Solving
General Education courses have been instrumental in broadening my perspective and equipping me with versatile skills. ARCH 321: History of the American City offered insight into how economic, cultural, and technological forces shape urban environments. ENGL 150 enhanced my writing and analytical skills, helping me articulate complex ideas logically and concisely.

2. The Role of First-Year Seminars
U ST 105 and U ST 106 (Carver Academy Seminars) introduced me to college life and taught valuable soft skills such as teamwork, time management, and leadership. These became crucial in labs and group projects for my computer engineering coursework.

3. Communication in Multiple Contexts
ENGL 250H challenged me to communicate using written, oral, visual, and electronic media. Whether presenting microcontroller project data or writing technical reports, I learned to adapt my communication for various audiences—a key skill in the fast-paced world of engineering.

Part II: Cumulative Reflection on the Iowa State Experience

1. Embracing Technical Coursework
My CprE classes formed the core of my technical skillset, covering digital logic design, embedded systems, and microprocessor architecture. In these courses, I learned to:

  • Design hardware using logic gates, FPGAs, and microcontrollers
  • Develop efficient software in both high- and low-level languages
  • Analyze trade-offs and optimize performance for real-time systems
One standout experience was my senior design project: building a social media apparel app that used AI to recommend similar and cheaper outfits—blending coding, cloud computing, data analysis, and client-facing skills.

2. The Big Picture of Iowa State Preparation
My ISU journey taught me to:

  • Design with societal impact in mind
  • Consider ethical and cultural dimensions in engineering problems
  • Thrive in collaborative, team-based environments
  • Communicate effectively across technical and non-technical contexts
  • Continuously seek new knowledge and tools
  • Analyze ethical responsibilities like privacy and social impact

3. Utilizing External Sources of Information
For advanced assignments, I used datasheets, developer forums, and scholarly articles to understand cutting-edge technologies. This practice strengthened my ability to stay up-to-date and apply new knowledge professionally.

4. Learning Activities Outside the Classroom

  • Hackathons: Practiced rapid prototyping and teamwork
  • Carver Academy: Fostered leadership and service
  • Internships: Applied technical knowledge to real-world problems

5. Continuous Learning and Skill Development
My time at ISU reinforced the need for lifelong learning. Whether through certifications, conferences, or personal projects, I’ve learned to remain agile in a constantly evolving field.

6. If I Could Redo My Undergraduate Experience…

  • Explore more interdisciplinary projects with design/business majors
  • Build closer faculty mentorships
  • Seek more global and multicultural exposure

Conclusion

My journey at Iowa State University was more than just technical training. Courses like ARCH 321, ENGL 150, ENGL 250H, and U ST 105/106 gave me a well-rounded foundation. As I move forward, I know that solving engineering problems requires not only logic and code, but empathy, ethics, and the ability to learn constantly. These principles will guide me as I contribute meaningfully to both industry and society.
